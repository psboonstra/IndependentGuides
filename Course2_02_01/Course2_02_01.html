Lets first inspect some of the variables in these data. The distribution of systolic blood pressure (systolic) in these data can be constructed as such:

ggplot(BPUrban300) + 
  geom_histogram(aes(systolic), bins = 30)

We can also inspect the relationship between systolic blood pressure and the age of the person:

ggplot(BPUrban300) + 
  geom_point(aes(age, systolic))

Linear models in R

We will now fit a linear regression model, regressing systolic against age to estimate the extent to which typical values systolic blood pressure change with a persons age in these data. We will use the lm function in R to do so.

The regression equation is specified via the formula argument in lm. A formula starts with the name of a variable that is intended to be the outcome. Next comes a tilde (~) symbol. Finally, it ends with the name of a variable that is intended to be the covariate / predictor. So, our formula will be systolic ~ age. We also need to point lm to the dataset in which to look for these variables in our case that is BPUrban300. Putting this together, we can fit our regression model with the following:

lm(formula = systolic ~ age, data = BPUrban300)
## 
## Call:
## lm(formula = systolic ~ age, data = BPUrban300)
## 
## Coefficients:
## (Intercept)          age  
##     93.8760       0.5371

The output gives us just two things: the Call, which just gives back the code that we gave it, and the Coefficients, which gives the intercept parameter (\(\beta_0\) from Dr.Mukherjees slides) and the slope parameter corresponding to age (\(\beta_1\)). Importantly, this tells us that the expected systolic blood pressure increases by about 0.5 points between any two people in our data who are 1 year apart in age.
